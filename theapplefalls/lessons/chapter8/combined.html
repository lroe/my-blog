Chapter 8, Lesson 1: The Two Definitions
The Direction of Flow

When you close a circuit, energy begins to move. But which way does it "flow"? This simple question has a complicated history.

In physics, we define Current ($I$) as the hypothetical flow of positive charge. This is known as "Conventional Current."

In the mid-1700s, Benjamin Franklin imagined electricity as a fluid that moved from an area of "excess" (positive) to an area of "deficit" (negative). It was a 50/50 guess, and as we'll see, the physics of solids eventually proved him wrong‚Äîbut not before his convention stuck forever.

Chapter 8, Lesson 2: The Drift Paradox
Chaos vs. Progress

In a copper wire, electrons are not sitting still. At room temperature, they zip around at a staggering million meters per second ($10^6$ m/s).

This "Thermal Speed" is chaotic and goes in all directions. On average, the electron goes nowhere.

But apply a small electric field ($E$), and a subtle change occurs. Amidst the chaos, the entire electron cloud begins to "drift."

Chapter 8, Lesson 5: The Materials Spectrum
The Range of Nature

Nature offers a range of resistivities so vast it is hard to comprehend. From the best conductors to the best insulators, the difference is a factor of $10^{22}$.

Recall our first-principles formula:
$\sigma = \frac{ne^2\tau}{m}$

The secret to this massive spectrum lies almost entirely in $n$‚Äîthe density of free charge carriers.
