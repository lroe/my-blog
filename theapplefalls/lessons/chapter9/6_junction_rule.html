Chapter 9: The Junction Rule

The Splitting Stream

What happens when a wire branches into two? Electrons are like water in a pipe—they don't just vanish.

Every electron that enters a junction (a node) must leave it through one of the branches.

Conservation of Charge

Kirchhoff's Junction Rule is Conservation of Charge in action.

The total current entering a node must equal the total current leaving it:

\(\sum I_{in} = \sum I_{out}\)

Path of Least Resistance

When current reaches a split, it doesn't split 50/50 unless the resistors are equal.

Try this: Lower one resistance and watch how more "particles" choose that path. The node acts as a traffic director based on ease of flow.

Verifying the Sum

Check the meters on the right. No matter how you adjust the sliders, you'll see:

\(I_{total} = I_1 + I_2\)

The total flow is always preserved!

Conclusion

In this simulation, we see discrete "balls" travel from the battery around the loop. In reality, it doesn't work like that.

The entire wire is already full of electrons. Turning on the battery is like pushing a bicycle chain: as soon as you push one link, the whole chain moves at once.

Current is the simultaneous drift of trillions of charges already present everywhere in the metal.
