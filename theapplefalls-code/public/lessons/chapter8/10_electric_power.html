Chapter 8: Electric Power

Energy on the Move

We know Potential Difference ($V$) is energy per unit charge. When these charges flow as Current ($I$), they are carrying energy through the circuit.

But how fast is that energy being transferred? That's what we call Power.

The Power Formula

By definition, Power is the rate of doing work. In SI units, the unit of power is the Watt (W), named after James Watt.

$$P = \frac{dU}{dt} = \frac{dU}{dq} \cdot \frac{dq}{dt}$$

Since $\frac{dU}{dq}$ is $V$ and $\frac{dq}{dt}$ is $I$, we get the most famous equation in electronics:

$$P [W] = V [V] \cdot I [A]$$

Joule Heating

In a resistor, this energy isn't "stored"—it's released as Heat. Modern physics calls this Joule Heating.

If we use Ohm's Law ($V = IR$), we can write the power dissipated as:

$$P = I^2 R = \frac{V^2}{R}$$

Conclusion

Observe the resistor in the simulation. As you increase the voltage or decrease the resistance, watch the power climb.

This "heat" is actually clear evidence that energy is being converted. But if a circuit is constantly losing energy to heat, how does it keep running?

It needs a source. It needs a pump. (Link to Chapter 9)
