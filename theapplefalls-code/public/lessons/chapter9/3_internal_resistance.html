Chapter 9: Internal Resistance

The "Missing" Energy

Until now, we've treated batteries as ideal. We assumed if a battery says 12V, it gives 12V no matter what.

But real life is messy. If you measure a battery when it's powering something heavy, you'll find the voltage is actually lower than advertised.

Internal Resistance

A battery is made of real materials—chemicals and metals—that have their own resistance.

We model this by putting a tiny resistor \(r_i\) inside the battery. We call the "pure" voltage the **EMF** (\(\mathcal{E}\)).

\(\mathcal{E} = 12V \text{ (Ideal Pump)}\)

The Terminal Voltage

When current flows, some energy is lost heating up the battery itself as it overcomes \(r_i\).

The voltage you actually measure at the terminals (\(V\)) is the EMF minus the "lost" voltage:

\(V = \mathcal{E} - I \cdot r_i\)

The "Voltage Sag"

Watch the voltmeter on the right. As you decrease the Load Resistance, the current increases.

The more current you draw, the more voltage is "stolen" by the internal resistor, causing the terminal voltage to sag.

Conclusion

What happens if you bridge the terminals with a wire (\(R_{load} \approx 0\))?

The current becomes massive: \(I = \mathcal{E} / r_i\). All that energy is dumped directly into the battery, turning into **intense heat**.

This internal friction is why batteries get hot—and can even leak or catch fire if the "sag" is too deep!
