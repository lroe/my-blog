Chapter 9: Maximum Power Transfer

The Power Dilemma

If you want to get the most power out of a battery, what should you do?

Decreasing the load resistance (\(R\)) gives you **more current**, but it also makes the terminal voltage **sag**! It's a trade-off.

A Bitter Short

At \(R = 0\), you have maximum current but zero voltage. Power (\(P=VI\)) is **zero**.

At infinite \(R\), you have maximum voltage but zero current. Power is **zero** again!

The "sweet spot" must be somewhere in between.

The Power Formula

Mathematically, power delivered to the load is \(P = I^2 R\).

\(P(R) = \left( \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R+r_i} \right)^2 R = \frac{\mathcal{E}^2 R}{(R+r_i)^2}\)

To find the maximum, we need to solve for when the slope is zero: \(\frac{dP}{dR} = 0\).

The Calculus

Using the quotient rule:

\(\frac{dP}{dR} = \mathcal{E}^2 \frac{(R+r_i)^2(1) - R(2)(R+r_i)}{(R+r_i)^4}\)

Setting numerator to zero:
\((R+r_i) - 2R = 0 \implies \mathbf{R = r_i}\)

This confirms the Matching Condition for maximum power.

Experiment

Use the sliders below to adjust the parameters. Notice how changing the source voltage (\(\mathcal{E}\)) scale the peak, but never moves its location!

The location of peak power is strictly determined by the internal resistance \(r_i\).

Conclusion

Matching \(R = r_i\) gives you max power, but it's only **50% efficient**. Half the energy is wasted heating the battery itself!

In power grids, we don't want max power—we want high efficiency, so we keep \(R\) much larger than \(r_i\).
